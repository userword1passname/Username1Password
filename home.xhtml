free and open content, data, information, knowledge

definitions and links

From the initial definitions below for each of free and open, in combination I gather the importance of both: 1. “freely by anyone for any purpose;” and 2. the likes of “can be” (in alphabetical order) “applied, copied, modified, shared, studied, and/or used.”

definition of free

The moderators at freedomdefined.org “define ‘Free Cultural Works’ as works or expressions which can be freely studied, applied, copied and/or modified, by anyone, for any purpose.”[1]

  • Definition of Free Cultural Works
definition of open

                         

The Open Knowledge Foundation’s “most succinct” summary of its Open Definition is: “Open data and content can be freely used, modified, and shared by anyone for any purpose”[2]

Jainism

thoughts

अपरिग्रह (aparigraha, non-attachment) is one of “the main religious premises of Jainism,”[3] and may very well have something to do with their release of the practitioner from obligation to a creator deity (like Brahmā or Jesus). To me, free and open knowledge, in its promotion of its freedom of utility by anyone for any purpose, allows the utilitarian to proceed according to their own karma, without having to answer to the source of the knowledge. I’ve worked with a number of steps in the direction of personal liberation, my step prior to this one being in connection with the Rosicrucians.

They have a principle called the Law of AMRA, which essentially has social directional reversal with the law of karma. Karma means that if you did something to someone, the universe might later find someone who is looking to do something similar, and might then judge that you are the appropriate target for said deed, given that you had behaved likewise previously. Now, this can be a good thing or a bad thing, but it has a type of sequence to it.

AMRA has a quantum level of interpersonal responsibility above karma in its prescription. In its polite form, it says that if someone (or if the universe in general) has done something to advantage you, you should then give to others in some way(s) as will perpetuate the interpersonal advantageousness. However, as highly virtuous as this is, it has been customarily associated with limitations to simple personal freedom, such freedoms for instance as might appeal to the śramaṇa mentality of the Jainist, who seeks to regain self-reliance above some of the more elaborate rules of etiquette amongst theists.

Rosicrucianism may be praised for its ethical and theistic intermediacy between śramaṇism and brāhmaṇism in allowing the human mystic to find their own sense of higher truth, but it applies many peer level rules to the practitioner, such as however a Jainist might yield for the sake of even truer meditation on what virtue is. I think this compares to the attribution expected of the carrier of acquired knowledge in modern American (and global) culture, where the origination of the medium and its formal attribution may be likened to the codes of religious worship obeying one of our traditional creator deities.

Virtue is a very real principle that pertains to treating life exactly how life likes to be treated, but the mandates of interpersonal, even cyclical, value carriage and enablement can be a distraction to the more immediate quality of attention used to strengthen truer virtue. Elaborative principles are great for a more ceremonial and ritualistic lifestyle, but what, in the given moment, are we actually using anything for? That is a very qualitatively important question, and there are still a great many transactions that can be accomplished by those who live their lives simply observing the likes of the laws of karma.

If you use something the wrong way, life might turn around and treat you wrong in reaction to that negligence. If you are, however, using what you have, including what you’ve acquired, to help your fellows and society, then more likely all will go well. Be trustworthy, and thereby trust your own way of doing whatever it is that you have chosen to be doing.
